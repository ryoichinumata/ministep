How to Start Tiny Habits That Actually Stick

Diet, studying, exercise, journaling.
You're motivated — but somehow it only lasts a few days.
What you need isn't "stronger willpower." You need better habit design.

Quitting early isn't a willpower problem

When starting something new, most people set big ambitious goals: "study one hour every day," "run every night."

But with work, chores, energy fluctuations, and life in general,
maintaining the same effort every single day is genuinely hard.

Still, we blame ourselves — thinking it's our weak willpower — when the real issue is the design.

The secret of people who stick with things

People who stick with habits for the long term don't start out doing it perfectly.
Their common trait: they set the starting bar incredibly low.

  • Not "read 30 minutes" — just "read 1 page"
  • Not "run 5km" — just "put on running shoes and step outside"
  • Not "study English for an hour" — just "open the textbook"

It might sound almost silly — but getting to a point where you think "I guess I could do that" is the first real step of habit formation.

3 principles for designing a tiny habit

  1. Keep it under 3 minutes
    No matter how busy you are, 3 minutes is almost always doable.
  2. Make the action specific
    Not "study" — but "open the textbook to page 10." Get that specific.
  3. Attach it to a trigger
    "After brushing teeth," "after pouring coffee" — link it to something you already do.

Tiny habit examples you can start today

  • Before bed, write down just one good thing from today
  • Right after waking up, open the curtains and take 3 deep breaths
  • Put just one item on your desk back in its place
  • Open the book you want to study — just open it and leave it on the desk
  • Whisper one encouraging word to yourself in your head

The key is that it's okay if it feels almost meaninglessly small.
It still makes a far bigger difference than doing nothing.
