How to Stop Procrastinating with the 'Just Start' Method

Procrastination isn't a character flaw — it's your brain's way of avoiding discomfort, uncertainty, or fear of failure. The good news is that a single tiny action is often all it takes to break the cycle and get moving.

Why we procrastinate in the first place

Most procrastination isn't about laziness. It's an emotional response — we avoid tasks because they trigger feelings of anxiety, boredom, self-doubt, or overwhelm. Understanding the root cause is the first step to dismantling it.

  • Fear of failure: the task feels high-stakes, so not starting feels safer
  • Perfectionism: you can't begin until conditions are "just right"
  • Overwhelm: the task feels too big to know where to start
  • Boredom: the work feels dull and your brain seeks stimulation elsewhere
  • Unclear next action: you don't actually know what "doing the task" means concretely

What the 'Just Start' method actually means

The Just Start method is deceptively simple: instead of committing to finishing a task, you only commit to beginning it. You decide in advance that you only need to do the first two minutes — and you give yourself full permission to stop after that. The trick is that once you start, momentum usually carries you forward.

  • Commit to two minutes, not the whole task
  • Eliminate the pressure of completion before you begin
  • Use a physical cue — opening a document, sitting at your desk — as the start signal
  • Tell yourself: "I'm just looking at this, not finishing it"
  • Recognize that starting is the hardest part, and plan accordingly

Making tasks tiny enough to actually start

If a task still feels too big to start, the answer is almost always to shrink it further. The goal isn't to trick yourself — it's to find the genuinely small action that is the real first step, then focus only on that.

  • Ask: "What is the very first physical action this task requires?"
  • Replace "write the report" with "open a blank document and type one sentence"
  • Replace "clean the house" with "clear one surface for three minutes"
  • Replace "exercise" with "put on your workout clothes"
  • Write the micro-task down the night before so there's no decision cost in the morning

Harnessing momentum once you've started

Newton's first law applies to productivity: a body in motion stays in motion. Once you've completed your tiny first step, you'll often find you want to keep going. The discomfort that made you avoid the task usually disappears within a minute or two of actually doing it.

  • After your two-minute start, ask: "Can I do two more minutes?"
  • Track your starts, not just completions — every start is a win
  • End each session at a natural pause point so starting next time is easy
  • Celebrate the act of beginning, not just finishing
  • Use a simple tally to count how many tasks you started today
