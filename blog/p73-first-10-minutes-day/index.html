Morning habits

What to Do in the First 10 Minutes of Your Day

How you spend the first ten minutes after waking up has a disproportionate effect on how calm, focused, and motivated you feel for the rest of the day. The choices you make in that small window — even if you're barely conscious — shape your nervous system's baseline state for hours.

The Morning Phone Trap

The single most common thing people do in the first ten minutes of their day is reach for their phone. It feels harmless — just a quick check of messages, the news, social media. But this habit immediately floods your brain with external demands, comparisons, and stress before your mind has had a chance to fully wake up and settle into itself.

  • Checking email or news within minutes of waking triggers a cortisol stress response
  • Social media comparisons in the morning set a negative emotional baseline for the day
  • Reactive phone use puts you in "response mode" rather than "intention mode" from the start
  • The dopamine hit from notifications trains your brain to crave constant stimulation
  • Studies show phone-free mornings are linked to higher reported daily satisfaction and productivity

How the First 10 Minutes Set Your Nervous System State

During sleep, your prefrontal cortex — the brain's center for rational thinking and emotional regulation — goes partially offline. In the first minutes after waking, it's still coming back online. Whatever input you receive during this window has an outsized influence on your emotional tone because your brain's filtering systems are not yet fully engaged.

  • The brain is in a hypnagogic-adjacent state for 10-20 minutes after waking, highly receptive to suggestion
  • Calm, low-stimulation inputs in this window reinforce the parasympathetic (rest) nervous system
  • Stressful inputs immediately activate the sympathetic (fight-or-flight) response
  • The tone set in the first 10 minutes can persist for 2-4 hours without a conscious reset
  • Positive morning emotions predict higher energy and better decision-making throughout the day

10-Minute Morning Options That Set a Positive Tone

You don't need an elaborate routine. You just need ten intentional minutes. Here are proven options — pick one or two that resonate with your personality and current lifestyle, rather than forcing someone else's perfect morning on yourself.

  • Drink a full glass of water before anything else — rehydration improves alertness within minutes
  • Open a window or step outside for two minutes of natural light and fresh air
  • Write three sentences in a journal: one thing you remember from a dream, one thing you're grateful for, one thing you want to accomplish today
  • Do five minutes of gentle stretching or simple movement to signal wakefulness to your body
  • Sit quietly with your morning drink and do nothing — just notice sounds, light, and how your body feels
  • Read one page of a book you're genuinely enjoying

Designing Your Personal 10-Minute Window

The best morning practice is the one you'll actually do consistently. Think about what kind of morning person you want to become — calm and reflective, or energized and ready to act — and design your first ten minutes around that vision. Small choices compound over time into a fundamentally different relationship with your mornings.

  • Choose actions that feel gentle, not performative — your morning should serve you, not impress others
  • Prepare the night before: set out your journal, fill your water bottle, charge your phone in another room
  • Protect the 10-minute window by setting a second alarm as a "phone-allowed" cue if needed
  • Be flexible on difficult days — even two phone-free minutes is better than zero
  • Use a MiniStep daily challenge as your structured 10-minute morning anchor to build the habit with guidance
